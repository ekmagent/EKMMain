### CHAPTER 1: What Plan G and Plan N Actually Cover (0:50)

First, what's the same. Both Plan G and Plan N cover the Medicare Part A hospital deductible — that's $1,676 per benefit period in 2026 — in full. Both cover Part A coinsurance plus an additional 365 days of hospital care after Medicare benefits run out. Both cover Part A hospice coinsurance. Both let you see any doctor or hospital in the country that accepts Original Medicare — no networks, no referrals. That's true of both plans, and it's the biggest advantage Medigap has over Medicare Advantage.

Both also cover foreign travel emergencies the same way: after a $250 deductible, they pay 80% of billed charges for emergency care during the first 60 days of a trip abroad, up to a $50,000 lifetime maximum. Identical on both plans.

[ON SCREEN: side-by-side coverage table]

Now the three differences — and there are only three.

One: the Part B deductible. That's $283 in 2026. Plan G covers it. Plan N does not — you pay that $283 out of pocket each year.

Two: Part B excess charges. If a doctor doesn't accept Medicare assignment, they can charge up to 15% above the Medicare-approved rate. Plan G covers that. Plan N does not.

Three: copays. Plan N has a copay of up to $20 per doctor visit, and up to $50 per emergency room visit if you're not admitted. Plan G has neither. Zero copays.

That's the whole list. Everything else is identical.

### CHAPTER 2: The Break-Even Math (3:00)

Here's where I want you to actually run numbers, because this is a math problem, not a preference problem.

In New Jersey, Plan N typically costs $20 to $40 less per month than Plan G for the same age and the same carrier. Call it $240 to $480 a year in premium savings.

[ON SCREEN: "Plan N saves $240–$480/year in premium"]

Against that savings, Plan N charges you back: $283 for the Part B deductible you now pay yourself, up to $20 per office visit, up to $50 per ER visit, and exposure to excess charges.

So here's the break-even: to come out ahead on Plan N, you'd need fewer than roughly 12 to 24 doctor visits per year, with no excess charges and no ER visits. If you see doctors regularly — specialists, follow-ups, the normal cadence of healthcare in your seventies and eighties — Plan G usually wins on total cost.

Where Plan N clearly wins: you're 65 to 70, in excellent health, you see your primary care doctor twice a year, and you rarely see specialists. In that case the premium savings just accumulate, year after year, without copays eating them.

Quick word on excess charges, because people panic about these. In New Jersey, most doctors accept Medicare assignment, so excess charges are uncommon here — but not impossible. If you frequently see out-of-state specialists, Plan G's excess charge coverage has more real value for you.

### CHAPTER 4: New Jersey's Community Rating — Why It Changes the Calculation (8:00)

New Jersey has something most states don't. In most states, Medigap premiums are attained-age rated — they go up every year just because you got a year older. In New Jersey, community rating means the insurer sets one premium for everyone — your premium does not increase because you aged.

[ON SCREEN: "NJ community rating: your premium doesn't rise because you got older"]

Two consequences for the G versus N decision.

First, the $20-to-$40 monthly savings from Plan N stays consistent as you age. In an attained-age state, that math shifts over time. In New Jersey, it's stable — so the decision really is about your healthcare usage pattern, not about a moving target.

Second — and this is the one people miss — switching later isn't free. You can apply to switch from Plan N to Plan G at any time, but outside your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period, insurers can use medical underwriting. If you're healthy, switching is usually straightforward. If your health has changed, it may be difficult or impossible. So the plan you pick at 65 might be the plan you're holding at 80. Pick it with your long-term health picture in mind, not just this year's premium.

And to be clear on the rules, because I hear this stated wrong constantly: your protected window is the 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period that starts the month you're 65 and enrolled in Part B. During those six months, New Jersey insurers must sell you any Medigap plan they offer, no health questions. New Jersey does not have year-round guaranteed issue. After that window, outside of specific federal guaranteed-issue situations, underwriting applies.

### CHAPTER 5: Who Should Pick Which (10:15)

Let me give you the honest decision framework.

Pick Plan G if: you see doctors regularly, you want zero surprises — no copays, no deductible math beyond the $283 Part B deductible, no excess-charge exposure — or you have any reason to think your health usage will go up. For most New Jersey residents, Plan G is the better choice. The $20-to-$40 monthly savings from Plan N rarely offsets the copays and gaps.

Pick Plan N if: you're 65 to 70, in excellent health, you see your primary doctor a couple times a year, you rarely see specialists, and you're genuinely comfortable paying $20 at the office and $50 at the ER when they happen. For that person, Plan N is a legitimate, rational way to save a few hundred dollars a year.

Either way — and this is the part that actually moves money — shop the carrier, not just the letter. A mid-priced Plan G beats an overpriced Plan N, and the cheapest filed Plan N at $106.57 beats a lot of Plan G quotes people accept without comparing.
