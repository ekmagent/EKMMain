### CHAPTER 1: Four Companies, One Brand (0:55)

When you get a Mutual of Omaha Medigap quote, your policy is issued by one of four legally separate insurance companies, depending on which state you live in:

- Mutual of Omaha Insurance Company — NAIC 71412, established 1966.
- United of Omaha Life Insurance Company — NAIC 69868, established 2008.
- Omaha Insurance Company — NAIC 13100, established 2012.
- Omaha Supplemental Insurance Company — NAIC 16537, established 2019.

[ON SCREEN: four-entity grid with NAIC codes]

All four carry the same AM Best A+ Stable financial strength rating — that part is consistent, and it's genuinely strong. But they have different NAIC company codes, different rate-filing histories, and different loss-ratio trajectories. In my review: Pennsylvania policies come from United of Omaha. Ohio from Omaha Supplemental. North Carolina from Omaha Insurance Company. Texas from Mutual of Omaha Insurance Company itself.

Why does that matter to you? Because the brand reputation is uniform, but your actual rate-increase experience depends on which of these four entities holds your policy — and as you're about to see, those experiences vary a lot.

One more structural fact before the numbers: every Mutual of Omaha entity uses attained-age pricing. Your premium rises every year as you age, on top of any filed rate increase. Over a 15-to-20-year Medigap tenure, that compounds dramatically versus issue-age or community-rated alternatives.

### CHAPTER 2: The Loss-Ratio Lens (3:00)

Quick tool for reading everything that follows. Loss ratio is claims paid divided by premiums collected. Near or above 100%, the carrier is losing money on that block, and a rate increase filing is coming. When rates jump, healthy policyholders shop out and switch, the remaining pool skews sicker, and the cycle repeats.

Here's the headline for the Mutual of Omaha family: loss ratios are running 95% to 97% in four of the five states I reviewed — even after three years of rate hikes. That tells me pricing has not caught up to claims experience, and more increases are likely.

[ON SCREEN: "Family loss ratios 2025: 95–97% in 4 of 5 states — after the rate hikes"]
