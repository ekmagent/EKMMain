### CHAPTER 1: The 6-Month Open Enrollment Period — Your One Protected Window (1:00)

Your Medigap Open Enrollment Period starts the month you are both 65 or older AND enrolled in Medicare Part B, and runs for six consecutive months. Both conditions. If you're 68 and just now leaving employer coverage and starting Part B, your window starts then.

During those six months, every carrier licensed in your state must accept your application regardless of your health history, and they cannot charge you a higher premium because of a pre-existing condition. No health questions that can hurt you. No rate-ups. Every carrier, every plan they offer.

[ON SCREEN: "OEP = 6 months from the month you're BOTH 65+ AND enrolled in Part B"]

Two things people get wrong constantly.

First: this is a one-time window. It is not annual. It does not reset.

Second: it is not the same thing as the Medicare Open Enrollment you see advertised every fall. That October 15 to December 7 period is for Medicare Advantage and drug plans. Medigap runs on its own clock — the six months tied to your age and your Part B start date. Confusing these two is probably the most expensive calendar mistake in all of Medicare.

### CHAPTER 2: The Lock-In-Early Play — Apply Up to 6 Months BEFORE Part B Starts (3:00)

Here's the part almost nobody tells you, and it's the reason I made this video.

Most carriers will accept a Medigap application up to six months in advance of your coverage starting. So if your Part B effective date is July 1st, you don't wait until July — you can apply in the spring, have everything approved and in place, and your coverage simply begins when Part B does.

Why does applying early matter? Two reasons.

One: it locks in your current-age rate. You apply at the age you are when you apply. Applying early rather than late in the process means the rate you lock reflects that earlier point — and with carriers that price by age, that matters.

Two: it removes the panic. The most common mistake I see is people waiting until the last month of their window and rushing through a single insurer's website. It works — but it removes your ability to compare quotes at a calm pace, and remember, the spread for identical coverage is $500-plus a month in a typical state. Rushing that comparison is expensive.

[ON SCREEN: "Most carriers accept applications up to 6 months BEFORE your Part B effective date"]

My practical timeline: start the process three to six months before your Part B effective date. Compare carriers in month one, apply in month two, and spend the rest of the time not thinking about it. That's the whole strategy.

### CHAPTER 3: What Happens If You Miss the Window (5:15)

Now the honest downside case. If your six-month OEP closes and you apply later, you don't lose the ability to buy Medigap — you lose the guaranteed acceptance.

After OEP, carriers can ask health questions, review your medical records, pull a prescription history report, and then do one of four things: approve you at standard rates, charge you a rate-up, impose a pre-existing condition look-back period, or decline you entirely. No medical exam — no Medigap carrier requires an in-person exam, it's a questionnaire — but your answers count now. Some applicants still qualify at standard rates. A significant minority lose access to the cheapest carriers entirely.

[ON SCREEN: "After OEP: underwriting. Approve / rate-up / look-back / decline."]

Processing speed changes too. Inside your OEP or a guaranteed-issue window, the application can be completed in a single phone call and most carriers approve within a few business days. With underwriting, it typically takes one to three weeks.

This is why the window is the single most consequential decision in Medigap shopping. The plan you can get at 65 with no questions may not be available to you at 70 at any price.

### CHAPTER 4: The Federal Guaranteed-Issue Triggers — Your Fallback Rights (7:00)

If you're watching this and your OEP is already gone — you may still have protected rights. Federal law forces carriers to accept you, with no health-based rate-ups, if you hit a guaranteed-issue trigger:

- You lose employer or union group health coverage.
- Your Medicare Advantage plan leaves your service area or shuts down.
- Your Medigap carrier goes bankrupt or commits fraud.
- The Medicare Advantage trial right — you joined MA for the first time and want to switch back within 12 months.
- You move out of your plan's service area.

[ON SCREEN: guaranteed-issue trigger list]

The catch: timing. Most guaranteed-issue windows are only 63 days from the qualifying event. Sixty-three days. If any of these just happened to you, the clock is already running.

A few states add extra protections on top of the federal floor — New York and Connecticut have year-round community-rated access, Missouri has an anniversary rule, California and Oregon have birthday rules. But most states — including my home state of New Jersey — follow the standard federal rules only: the 6-month OEP plus these guaranteed-issue triggers. New Jersey does not have year-round open enrollment, no matter what a Facebook comment told you.

### CHAPTER 5: The 5-Step Enrollment Process (9:00)

So the timing is settled. Here's the actual process, five steps.

Step one: confirm you have both Medicare Part A and Part B. You cannot buy Medigap without both. Part B costs $202.90 a month for most enrollees in 2026. Not enrolled yet? Start at ssa.gov/medicare.

Step two: compare every licensed carrier in your state — not one insurer's website. Benefits are federally standardized; a Plan G from any carrier covers exactly what a Plan G from any other covers. Premiums for that identical product vary by $500-plus a month. Applying direct to one insurer means seeing one price for a standardized product.

Step three: pick your plan letter. Plan G is the most comprehensive plan available to anyone newly eligible in 2026 — it covers everything except the $283 Part B deductible. Plan N is the lower-premium alternative with small office and ER copays. Plan F is closed to anyone who became eligible on or after January 1, 2020.

Step four: apply inside your 6-month window — or better, early, before Part B even starts, like we covered.

Step five: submit through a licensed broker rather than direct. Here's the fact that surprises everyone: Medigap commissions are regulated and built into every premium — the price is identical whether you apply direct, through a broker, or through a captive agent. There is no discount for going direct. The broker route just adds the comparison: every carrier's price at once, the household discount — that's 5 to 14% at most carriers, with eligibility rules that differ at each one — the rate-increase history, and the pricing methodology, whether a carrier is community-rated, issue-age, or attained-age. The cheapest Plan G today is not always the cheapest Plan G at 75.

[ON SCREEN: "Broker vs direct: same premium. The comparison is free."]

One housekeeping item: Medigap does not cover prescription drugs. You'll want a separate Part D drug plan, and you can enroll in both in the same conversation.
